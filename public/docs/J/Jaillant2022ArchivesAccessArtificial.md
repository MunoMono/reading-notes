**Chapter 3: Design Thinking, UX and Born-Digital Archives** (pp. 83–108),

These sections are most relevant to DDR’s RAG/XAI/provenance strand, illuminating:

- the infrastructural and ethical conditions of AI in archives.

## Purpose and aim
### What research question or objective is being addressed?

The volume seeks to answer:  
1. **How can AI help transform access to born-digital and digitised archives?**  
2. **What prevents researchers from using AI-enhanced archival collections at scale?**  
3. **How can design thinking, UX, provenance and critically addressable data frameworks support ethical AI deployment?**  
4. **How should archival science evolve to accommodate computational methods?**

The Introduction argues that traditional archival debates on selection and preservation are no longer sufficient; **access at scale** is now the primary challenge (p. 9).  [oai_citation:0‡full_text.pdf](sediment://file_00000000274471f490e7f2e9d2bfe45c)

Born-digital archives remain “dark,” not due to lack of content but due to **too much content and too little usable access** (pp. 8–10). AI is framed not as a speculative technology but as a **necessary component of modern archival infrastructures**.

## Methodology
### Describe the research design, methods and sample size.

Because this is an edited scholarly volume, the methodological basis is **plural and heterogeneous**, including:

- **Archival case studies** (e.g., national archives, literary archives).  
- **Design thinking & UX analysis** for archival interfaces (Chapter 3).  
- **Computational demonstrations** (AI discovery tools, clustering, neural nets).  
- **Critical data studies** focusing on metadata, representation and bias.  
- **Workflow audits** of Web archives, digitised collections, and born-digital repositories.  
- **Theoretical synthesis** in the CAS afterword.

Jaillant’s Chapter 3 applies **design-thinking frameworks** to user needs assessment, user journeys, and interface prototypes for “dark archives” (pp. 85–95).  
Gooding’s Chapter 4 uses **logical argumentation + empirical examples** (OCR noise, metadata drift, broken provenance) to define “critically addressable data” (pp. 109–112).  
The CAS afterword synthesises developments in AI, archival science, and DH into a conceptual field (pp. 205–215).

## Key findings and arguments
### Summarise the main results and conclusions

1. **Dark archives are the defining access problem of our era.**  
   - Born-digital archives remain invisible due to privacy, copyright, lack of metadata, missing UX design, and infrastructural neglect (pp. 9–10).  
   - Even when digitised materials exist, they often lack machine-readable structure, bulk access, or computational tooling.

2. **AI must be treated as an access layer, not a post-hoc add-on.**  
   - AI can support sensitivity review, pattern detection, entity extraction, and large-scale search (p. 10).  
   - But it must remain **transparent, auditable, and provenance-aware**, aligning with archival ethics.

3. **UX and design thinking provide a framework for human-centred AI in archives.**  
   - Chapter 3 argues that archives cannot rely on “build it and they will come”; they must conduct **user research, persona modelling, and workflow mapping** (pp. 83–86).  
   - Good archival UX should foreground **legibility, evidence visibility, and granular provenance** (pp. 92–96).

4. **Critically addressable data reframes archives as infrastructures requiring explanation.**  
   - Gooding argues that data needs to be **computationally tractable, provenance-rich, and ethically contextualised** to support meaningful AI work (pp. 110–113).  
   - OCR quality, missing metadata, digitisation decisions, and ordering systems must be documented for both human and machine use (pp. 118–123).

5. **AI can exacerbate archival inequalities unless carefully governed.**  
   - Bias in training data and descriptive practices risks amplifying existing archival silences (pp. 11–12).  
   - Users need transparency: why results appear, what is missing, and how algorithms rank relevance.

6. **CAS (Computational Archival Science) provides a scholarly home for AI/archives integration.**  
   - The afterword presents CAS as a field integrating archival theory, provenance tracking, ML, and systems design (pp. 205–207).  
   - CAS emphasises **interdisciplinarity, uncertainty modelling, provenance, reproducibility**, and human–AI collaboration.

7. **Metadata quality and rights infrastructures determine the limits of AI.**  
   - Copyright and privacy restrictions disproportionately affect born-digital archives (pp. 10–12).  
   - Without rights-aware AI tools and granular sensitivity metadata, access cannot scale.

8. **Digitisation bias shapes what becomes computationally visible.**  
   - Selective digitisation distorts the historical record AI can learn from (pp. 11–12).  
   - “Computational visibility” is politically and institutionally constructed.

## Interpretation
### Analysis and insights

- AI retrieval should be reframed as **archival description**, not mere computation.  
- Explainability is essential: DDR must reveal *why* results appear, what is missing, and what uncertainties exist.  
- Metadata quality is an epistemic issue: digitisation decisions shape AI outcomes.  
- DDR must include transparency dashboards showing OCR confidence, metadata completeness, provenance chains, and rights restrictions.  
- User-centred design is key: archival systems fail because they are built around constraints, not around researchers.  
- DDR can concretely contribute to CAS by demonstrating how RAG + provenance can coexist ethically and interpretively.

### Alternative explanations?
- Instead of lack of UX, some problems may be due to legal environments (GDPR, copyright) that no amount of design can offset.  
- AI enthusiasm risks overshadowing simpler improvements such as unified metadata fields and better rights statements.

### Implications for practice, policy or theory?
- DDR should embed provenance logs and tool-version tracking by design.  
- RAG outputs must include evidence lists, confidence scores and “why this?” explanations.  
- DDR can model a responsible AI framework for small institutional archives.
