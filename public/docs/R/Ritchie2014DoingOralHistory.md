# Scope of this note
Focuses on Chapters 3–6: conducting and setting up interviews; using oral history in research and writing; video oral history; and preserving interviews in archives and libraries. These chapters are mined for operational standards, ethical guardrails, and documentary practices that can be transposed into DDR’s mixed qualitative–computational workflow. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook pp. 100–235).

## Purpose and aim
### What research question or objective is being addressed?
Ritchie’s objective is pragmatic: to specify reliable, ethical, and reusable practice for oral history—from project scoping and interview conduct to editing, citation, access and preservation—so that interviews can bear scholarly scrutiny and public use. He addresses failures common in the field (e.g., weak documentation, poor releases, ad hoc archiving) and prescribes remedies that standardise quality and enable reuse. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook pp. 135–172, 201–235).

## Methodology
### Describe the research design, methods and sample size.
This is a practitioner’s handbook rather than a single empirical study. Evidence takes the form of exemplars, counter‑examples and procedural guidance drawn from Ritchie’s own interviewing (e.g., New Deal insiders) and from institutional collections (NASA, Shoah Foundation, libraries and museums). The method is normative and synthetic, grounded in field cases to derive prescriptions on planning, consent, recording media, transcription, indexing, citation, access and preservation. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook pp. 100–134; 135–172; 173–200; 201–235). 

## Key findings and arguments
### Summarise the main results and conclusions
1. **Plan with “actuarial realities.”** Prioritise the oldest and most central narrators; use gatekeepers to open networks; keep interviews to c. two hours to avoid fatigue. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 100–134, printed p. 5).
2. **Document consent properly.** Secure signed releases at the time of interview; IRB‑style “informed consent” should be satisfied by standard oral‑history releases tailored to risks. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed p. 5). 
3. **Design for reuse.** Anticipate “second‑generation” research; deposit interviews so later scholars can reinterpret beyond the original project’s aims. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed p. 17).
4. **Cite interviews rigorously.** Treat oral sources like manuscripts: identify interviewee, interviewer, format, date, and holding repository; include page/track/time references. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed pp. 32–33). fileciteturn8file6  
5. **Prefer video when it adds evidential value.** Video captures gesture, setting and multi‑speaker identification; it enables exhibition and documentary use, but requires careful ethics and higher preservation costs. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 173–200, printed pp. 1–2). 
6. **Beware post‑production distortion.** Editing can invert meaning; historians consulting on documentaries often lose control—hence the need for standards that balance drama with accuracy. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 173–200, printed p. 21). 
7. **Archive with consistency.** Arrange interviews systematically (usually by interviewee); create processing manuals; separate interview records from organisational paperwork; build robust finding aids and digital access. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 201–235, printed pp. 8–9). 
8. **Libraries as hubs.** Public libraries can coordinate community projects, volunteers, indexing and training, acting as clearinghouses with clear legal terms. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 201–235, printed pp. 31–33).
9. **Ethics online.** For vulnerable narrators, prefer delayed release or privacy‑preserving dissemination (e.g., audio without transcripts; first names only). [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed p. 19).

## Relevance
### How does it link to the research questions or framework?
The book supplies operational standards for DDR’s oral‑history strand: prioritisation, consent, media choice, indexing, citation, and repository practice. It legitimises “second‑generation” reuse—central to the project’s living‑archive ambition—and aligns with the plan to publish interviews into an API‑first platform with strict provenance. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172; 201–235).

## Project integration
### Why it helps the project (evidence-linked)
- **Interview sequencing and length.** Prioritise elder DDR figures and gatekeepers; default to ≤120‑minute sessions with scheduled breaks. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 100–134, printed p. 5).
- **Consent and risk model.** Use signed releases at interview; record options for anonymity and staged opening; store IRB‑style consent metadata alongside recordings. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed p. 5).
- **Repository deposit.** Mandate deposit with full metadata, transcripts and time‑indices to enable reinterpretation by others. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed p. 17).
- **Citation policy.** Enforce interview citation format in all outputs and platform exports; include repository and persistent identifiers. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 135–172, printed pp. 32–33).
- **Video strategy.** Use selective video where gesture, artefact‑handling or multi‑speaker identification are evidential; plan higher preservation costs. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 173–200, printed pp. 1–2).
- **Editing safeguards.** Log editorial interventions; publish segment‑level provenance to deter misrepresentation in derivatives. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 173–200, printed p. 21).  
- **Processing manual.** Create a DDR oral‑history processing guide covering arrangement, transcription standards, indexing and digital access. [@Ritchie2014DoingOralHistory] (ebook 201–235, printed pp. 8–9).

## Critical evaluation
### Strengths
- **Operational clarity.** Concrete, check‑listable guidance on sequencing, gatekeeping, length, and locating interviewees; easy to transpose into project SOPs. (ebook 100–134, printed p. 5).
- **Reuse‑orientation.** Strong on deposit, citation, indexing, and access—directly enabling second‑generation scholarship. (ebook 135–172, p. 17; 201–235, pp. 8–9). 
- **Media literacy.** Nuanced treatment of video’s evidential gains and post‑production risks. (ebook 173–200, pp. 1–2, 21).

### Weaknesses and limitations
- **Anglo‑American institutional bias.** Guidance assumes access to libraries, IRBs, and well‑resourced repositories; less applicable to precarious, transnational, or activist contexts. (inferred from emphasis on libraries/IRBs).  
- **Under‑theorised digital ethics.** The advice on online sensitivity is pragmatic but thin on contemporary platform harms or algorithmic search exposure. (ebook 135–172, p. 19).
- **Limited computational integration.** Indexing guidance is catalogue‑centric; less on graph models, entity extraction, or API‑level dissemination—gaps the project will have to fill. (ebook 201–235).

### Author’s credibility
Seasoned practitioner whose examples stem from high‑level political and institutional interviewing (e.g., New Deal figures), and whose prescriptions reflect decades of field experience and archival collaboration. (ebook 100–134, printed p. 5).

### Contextual validity
The standards generalise well to institutional oral‑history programmes, museums, and public libraries; the book’s casework demonstrates cross‑sector adoption. (ebook 173–200, pp. 1–2, 22; 201–235, pp. 31–33).

### Comparisons
Converges with Thompson’s emphasis on reflexivity and shared authority; complements Portelli on meaning‑making; extends Perks & Thomson on institutional practice. (conceptual triangulation; not text‑cited).

## Interpretation
### Analysis and insights
- **Design implication 1: provenance as product.** The platform should expose consent terms, editorial interventions and indexing provenance at segment level, answering Ritchie’s concern about distortion and citation rigour. (ebook 135–172, pp. 32–33; 173–200, p. 21).
- **Design implication 2: selective video.** Given cost, select video where gesture, artefact handling, or group dynamics are evidential; otherwise prioritise high‑quality audio with rich time‑codes. (ebook 173–200, pp. 1–2).
- **Design implication 3: second‑generation use.** Treat deposit, indexing and API exposure as first‑order outputs, not afterthoughts; this future‑proofs the corpus. (ebook 135–172, p. 17; 201–235, pp. 8–9).

- **Policy implication.** Adopt a two‑tier release policy: (i) embargoed deposit for sensitive material; (ii) public release with privacy‑preserving edits for vulnerable participants. (ebook 135–172, p. 19).

- **Theoretical implication.** Ritchie’s “use‑value” stance reframes oral history as infrastructural labour—procedures that make memory legible, citable and recombinable—aligning with the project’s living‑archive thesis.

### How does it shape your thinking?
It compels the project to treat workflows (consent, indexing, citation, deposit) as epistemic, not clerical. The measure of quality is not only narrative richness but also the capacity for verification, recombination and responsible public use—exactly the affordances the DDR platform promises to deliver.
